Why you should start planning now

Rio J. Barretto
June 08, 2015

I was young once. Too young to think about planning a future…or retirement?!! I was going to be a dot com millionaire by age 30….when the world was only just figuring out the internet. I would marry a rich guy…though I didn’t like the idea of being anyone’s trophy wife. Somewhere in between I even fantasized that I would be discovered as the face or sound of the new generation…never mind that I don’t have the looks or talent. Of course, none of these dreams came true. They were just dreams that a lot of youngsters have.

I also had goals. I wanted to get out of the small town I grew up in. I wanted to ace every exam. I wanted to wear designer clothes. I wanted to travel the world. I wanted to own a nice apartment. I wanted to be financially independent. I wanted to be an expert in something. I wanted to contribute to something important. I managed to achieve all of these. I am still living some of them. I cared about these dreams enough to figure out how to get them and work hard towards them.

Isn’t it interesting that anytime that I cared for something enough to make a plan, I got it? That’s the funny thing about dreams and goals. If you are really clear about what you want, you tend to figure out how to get there. When people travel, they plan their itinerary. When they want to become doctors, they study for the medical entrance exam. When they want to have babies, they ask their spouses ‘should we start planning?’

Whether big life goals or small wins, research shows that when people write down their goals, they are significantly more likely to achieve them. Indeed, motivational speakers ask you visualise them and put up pictures to remind you of the endgame when the going gets tough. Yes, luck plays a part. But ask successful people about luck and they will tell you – the harder you work, the luckier you get.

So when people ask me for investing tips, I ask them about their goals. A lot of times they say they want ‘maximum returns’ or ‘get rich’. I think they either have some money mindset issues driving them (see previous blog) or they haven’t met a good financial adviser (or of course, don’t want to discuss their goals in a social setting).

As a good financial adviser will tell you, most financial goals fall under the following categories –

  • Short term – living essentials, lifestyle and emergency cash
  • Medium term – lifestyle (house, car, travel etc), further/kids’ education
  • Long term – kids’ weddings, financial independence/retirement, legacy

Only when the financial adviser understands your goals, can he or she help you come up with an appropriate plan. The goals drive the plan’s investment objectives, timeframes, risk profiles, and constraints. If your goal is to buy a house in 2-3 years' time, you shouldn't invest in equities. If your goal is to be like Warren Buffett, you can't do without equities.

?In summary, there are three reasons why you should start planning now -

Reframe ‘retirement’ to ‘financially independent’ Simply reframe the word ‘retirement’to ‘financially independent’. It’s when your income from your investments is enough to have a great lifestyle. While you can still choose to work, youare more likely to work because you enjoy it rather than because you have to.

If you plan, you are more likely to achieve it Research shows that when people write down their goals, they are significantly more likely to achieve them. Indeed, drawing or putting up pictures of your goals motivates them even more, especially when the going gets tough.

The more time you have, the more compounding can work for you Albert Einstein is quoted as saying “Compound interest is the eighth wonder of the world. He who understands it, earns it…he who doesn’t, pays it.” Compound interest is ‘interest on interest’ – hence compounding refers to how much something is magnified because of this. If you are investing, your returns can get compounded over time. But for it to work its magic, you need to give it time. So the earlier you start, the more compounding can work for you.
