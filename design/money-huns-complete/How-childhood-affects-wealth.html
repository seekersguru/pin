How childhood affects wealth

Rio J. Barretto
June 08, 2015

“Don’t be so money-minded” - That’s the remark some women get when they take any interest in saving, managing or making money. “Money is evil” or “money is the root of all problems” are other such comments one hears often. In the book, Rich Dad Poor Dad, the author cites the effect of the different mindsets of his own poor father and his friend’s rich father. Irrespective of whether the book narrative is fictional or real, it is interesting to explore where such comments come from in the real world and whether they affect how people think and act in relation to their money.

The area of behavioural finance has developed quite a bit over the past few decades. Psychologists have been studying various aspects of how we relate to money. There are well-documented examples of how we don’t always act rationally when it comes to money. For example, we extrapolate more recent performance into the future, or we are over-confident. But the financial industry hasn’t necessarily taken clear action on how we can avoid those blind spots or whether being aware of these irrational behaviours makes us overcome them.

In the 2010 book Mind Over Money, the authors theorise that almost everyone suffers from money issues that prevent them from living up to her full potential. It’s not lack of financial education that keeps them from becoming wealthy, but deeply held psychological hang-ups about money. And most of these have their foundations in childhood experiences. The study comes up with a few ‘money disorders’ – disorders are not one-off events but persistent, predictable, often rigid patters of self-destructive financial behaviours that cause significant stress and anxiety. The disorders are usually one of money avoidance, money worshipping, money status and money vigilance.

  1. Money avoiders believe that money is bad or that they do not deserve money. Money is seen as a force that stirs up fear, anxiety or disgust. They often carry two seemingly contradictory money ‘scripts’ – ‘my self worth equals my net worth’, and ‘things would get better if I had more money’. Denial is the classic defense mechanism; trying our best to avoid thinking about money is an example of financial denial. Underspending is another symptom of money avoidance where savers hoard money, often based on fear or anxiety or guilt of being undeserving. In a subsequent study, money avoiders were found to have lower levels of income and net worth. Younger and single individuals were also more likely to be money avoiders.
  2. On the other hand, money-worshippers believe ‘money money will make things better’. They tend to equate money with safety, self-worth and happiness, placing a disproportionate amount of importance on earning, saving and spending money. Even though there is evidence that more money doesn’t necessarily lead to more happiness, money worshippers cling to the notion of more is better.
  3. ‘Money is status’ scripts link self worth with net worth, locking individuals into a competitive stance of acquiring more than those around them.
  4. The money vigilance script is about money being a deep source of shame and secrecy, whether one has a lot or a little. Culturally, Asians tend to be more private about their personal affairs as a form of self-protection.

As with any other therapy, the authors suggest becoming aware of these disorders by writing down earliest memories about money, and parents’ attitudes to money. They suggest resolving any unfinished business and then re-writing the scripts.

More recently published personal finance books are following the pattern taken by diet books – they advise dealing with the mindset before starting on a program. Alexa von Tobel, in her book Financially Fearless, starts by looking at the mindset. She suggests three good habits that apparently predict people’s success with money – organisation, thoughfulness (as opposed to impulsiveness) and independent thinking.

I agree. I think it's good to become more self-aware about your own money mindset, whether through discussion with family or financial adviser, or just being conscious of what pops into your head. It’s also good to seek out better role models if your own parents haven’t set the best example. Regulators are already talking about including basic concepts about money in school curricula – I hope it will encourage kids to discuss money more openly with their parents, and perhaps developing their own thinking.

So, please do think about money script. What are your earliest memories about money? How do you think about money now?
